Understanding Sleep Apnea

What Is Sleep Apnea — and How Serious Is It?

The Silent Nighttime Emergency

Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) occurs when the throat muscles relax during sleep, causing the airway to collapse or narrow. The result: breathing stops repeatedly throughout the night — sometimes hundreds of times — starving the brain and heart of oxygen.

Left untreated, sleep apnea significantly increases risk of hypertension, heart attack, stroke, type 2 diabetes, and depression. It's also a leading cause of daytime fatigue and road accidents.

  • ✓Affects an estimated 10–13% of India's adult population
  • ✓Over 80% of cases remain undiagnosed in India
  • ✓CPAP/BiPAP therapy is the gold-standard treatment — non-surgical, non-drug
CPAP vs BiPAP

Understanding the Difference

CPAP
Continuous Positive Airway Pressure

Delivers one continuous, steady pressure throughout both inhalation and exhalation. The standard, most-prescribed treatment for mild to moderate obstructive sleep apnea.

BiPAP
Bi-Level Positive Airway Pressure

Delivers higher pressure during inhalation (IPAP) and lower pressure during exhalation (EPAP). Preferred for severe OSA, central sleep apnea, and patients who find CPAP exhalation pressure difficult.

Who Needs CPAP or BiPAP Therapy?

Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)

The primary indication for CPAP. Patients diagnosed with mild to severe OSA through a sleep study (polysomnography) requiring nightly airway pressure support.

COPD & Respiratory Failure

BiPAP is commonly used for COPD exacerbations, neuromuscular disease, and hypercapnic respiratory failure requiring non-invasive ventilatory support.

Post-Operative & ICU Step-Down

Hospitals use BiPAP for patients being stepped down from mechanical ventilation or for post-surgical respiratory support before full extubation.
