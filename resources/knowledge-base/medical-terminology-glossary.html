Glossary of Respiratory and Home Care Medical Terms

Medical device specifications, clinical literature, and physician prescriptions use specialised terminology that can be confusing for patients and families. This glossary defines the most commonly encountered terms across respiratory therapy, nebulization, sleep therapy, wound care, and medical device regulation — explained in plain language with context for why each term matters.

Respiratory Therapy Terms

PSA (Pressure Swing Adsorption) — The technology used by oxygen concentrators to separate oxygen from ambient air. Two beds of zeolite molecular sieve alternately adsorb nitrogen under pressure, producing a continuous stream of 93% pure oxygen. PSA is the core technology that makes home oxygen concentrators possible without needing compressed gas cylinders.

LPM (Litres Per Minute) — The standard unit of measurement for oxygen flow rate. A physician's prescription will specify a flow rate in LPM (e.g., "2 LPM continuous flow"). Home concentrators typically deliver 0.5–5 LPM or 0.5–10 LPM depending on the model. Higher LPM means more oxygen is delivered per minute, but the appropriate rate depends on the patient's clinical need — more is not necessarily better.

SpO2 (Peripheral Oxygen Saturation) — The percentage of haemoglobin molecules in the blood that are carrying oxygen, measured non-invasively by a pulse oximeter clipped to the fingertip. Normal SpO2 in a healthy person is 95–100%. Oxygen therapy is typically prescribed when resting SpO2 falls consistently below 88%. This is the most commonly used metric for monitoring oxygen therapy effectiveness at home.

FiO2 (Fraction of Inspired Oxygen) — The concentration of oxygen in the air a patient is breathing, expressed as a decimal or percentage. Room air has an FiO2 of 0.21 (21%). An oxygen concentrator at 2 LPM through a nasal cannula delivers approximately FiO2 0.28 (28%). The FiO2 achieved depends on the flow rate, the delivery device (cannula vs. mask), and the patient's breathing pattern.

LTOT (Long-Term Oxygen Therapy) — The continuous or near-continuous use of supplemental oxygen for patients with chronic hypoxemia, typically prescribed for a minimum of 15 hours per day. Landmark clinical trials (NOTT, MRC) demonstrated that LTOT significantly reduces mortality in COPD patients with severe resting hypoxemia. LTOT is the primary clinical indication for purchasing a home oxygen concentrator.

Zeolite Molecular Sieve — A porous aluminosilicate mineral used inside oxygen concentrators to adsorb nitrogen from compressed air. Zeolite has a crystalline structure with pore sizes that preferentially trap nitrogen molecules (slightly larger) while allowing oxygen molecules to pass through. The zeolite beds are the most critical component affecting a concentrator's purity output and lifespan. Exposure to excessive moisture degrades zeolite performance over time.

Nasal Cannula — A lightweight, two-pronged plastic tube that sits in the patient's nostrils and delivers supplemental oxygen. It is the most common oxygen delivery interface for home therapy at flow rates of 1–6 LPM. Nasal cannulas allow patients to eat, drink, and speak while receiving oxygen. They should be replaced every 2–4 weeks for hygiene reasons.

Venturi Mask — A face mask with colour-coded jet adapters that deliver precise, fixed FiO2 concentrations (24%, 28%, 31%, 35%, 40%, or 60%) regardless of the patient's breathing pattern. Venturi masks are used when precise FiO2 control is important, such as in CO2-retaining COPD patients where excessive oxygen can suppress respiratory drive. Less commonly used at home than nasal cannulas.

Humidifier Bottle — A small water chamber (bubbler) attached to the oxygen outlet of a concentrator or cylinder regulator. Dry oxygen passing through the water picks up moisture, reducing nasal dryness, nosebleeds, and throat irritation that patients often experience with supplemental oxygen. Should be filled with distilled or sterile water (not tap water) and cleaned or replaced regularly to prevent bacterial growth.

Continuous Flow vs. Pulse Dose — Two modes of oxygen delivery. Continuous flow delivers a steady stream of oxygen at the set LPM rate, regardless of whether the patient is inhaling or exhaling. Pulse dose (intermittent flow) uses a sensor to detect inhalation and delivers a bolus of oxygen only during the breath-in phase, conserving oxygen supply. Continuous flow is required for sleep use and is the standard for home concentrators. Pulse dose is primarily used in portable concentrators to extend battery life, but is not suitable for all patients.

Nebulization and Aerosol Terms

MMAD (Mass Median Aerodynamic Diameter) — The particle size at which 50% of the aerosolised drug mass consists of particles larger than the stated diameter, and 50% consists of smaller particles. For effective lung deposition, the MMAD should be between 1 and 5 micrometres (microns). Particles larger than 5 microns deposit in the throat and mouth; particles smaller than 1 micron are exhaled without depositing. MMAD is the single most important specification for evaluating nebulizer effectiveness.

Respirable Fraction — The percentage of aerosolised drug particles that fall within the respirable range (typically 1–5 microns MMAD) and are therefore likely to reach the lower airways and lungs. A higher respirable fraction means more of the medication actually reaches its target. Quality nebulizers achieve a respirable fraction of 60–80%.

Aerosol — A suspension of fine liquid droplets or solid particles in a gas. In the context of nebulization, an aerosol is the mist of medication-containing droplets produced by the nebulizer and inhaled by the patient. The aerosol must be fine enough to penetrate deep into the bronchial tree.

Nebulization Rate (ml/min) — The volume of liquid medication converted into aerosol per minute. A typical compressor nebulizer produces 0.2–0.4 ml/min. Higher nebulization rates mean shorter treatment sessions, which is particularly important for children and elderly patients who may not tolerate prolonged nebulization. However, rate must be balanced against particle size — excessively fast nebulization can produce larger, less respirable particles.

Residual Volume — The amount of liquid medication that remains in the nebulizer cup after the device stops producing aerosol (the "dead volume"). This medication is wasted. Typical residual volumes range from 0.5 to 1.5 ml. A lower residual volume means more of the prescribed dose is actually delivered to the patient, which is particularly important for expensive medications.

Piezoelectric Transducer — A component in mesh nebulizers that vibrates at ultrasonic frequencies (typically 100–180 kHz) to force liquid medication through a fine mesh membrane, producing an aerosol. Piezoelectric nebulizers (also called vibrating mesh nebulizers) are quieter, more portable, and more efficient than traditional compressor nebulizers, but are more expensive and require careful cleaning of the mesh.

Venturi Effect — A principle of fluid dynamics used in jet (compressor) nebulizers. Compressed air is forced through a narrow orifice, creating a region of low pressure that draws liquid medication up through a feed tube and shears it into fine droplets. The Venturi effect is the operating principle behind most hospital and home compressor nebulizers.

MDI (Metered Dose Inhaler) — A pressurised canister that delivers a fixed dose of aerosolised medication with each actuation (puff). MDIs are the most commonly prescribed inhaler type for asthma and COPD maintenance therapy. They require coordination between pressing the canister and inhaling, which can be difficult for children and elderly patients — a limitation that nebulizers overcome by providing continuous aerosol during normal tidal breathing.

DPI (Dry Powder Inhaler) — An inhaler that delivers medication in dry powder form, activated by the patient's own inhalation effort. Unlike MDIs, DPIs do not require propellants or hand-breath coordination, but they do require a minimum inspiratory flow rate to disperse the powder effectively. Patients with severe airflow limitation may not generate sufficient flow to use a DPI effectively.

Spacer — A chamber (typically a plastic tube or cone) attached to an MDI that allows the aerosolised medication to decelerate and the propellant to evaporate before inhalation. Spacers significantly improve lung deposition by reducing oropharyngeal (throat and mouth) deposition, and they eliminate the need for precise hand-breath coordination. Spacers are strongly recommended for children, elderly patients, and anyone using inhaled corticosteroids.

Sleep Therapy Terms

AHI (Apnea-Hypopnea Index) — The number of apnea (complete breathing cessation) and hypopnea (partial breathing reduction) events per hour of sleep. AHI is the primary metric for diagnosing and classifying sleep apnea severity: mild (5–14 events/hour), moderate (15–29 events/hour), and severe (30+ events/hour). AHI is measured during a sleep study (polysomnography) and is also tracked nightly by CPAP/BiPAP devices to monitor treatment effectiveness.

CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure) — A therapy device that delivers a constant, fixed pressure of air through a mask to keep the upper airway open during sleep. CPAP is the first-line treatment for moderate to severe obstructive sleep apnea. The pressure is set by a sleep physician based on titration study results, typically ranging from 4 to 20 cmH2O. The device consists of a blower unit, humidifier, tubing, and a nasal or full-face mask.

BiPAP/BPAP (Bilevel Positive Airway Pressure) — A therapy device that delivers two different pressures: a higher pressure during inhalation (IPAP) and a lower pressure during exhalation (EPAP). This bilevel approach is more comfortable for patients who find exhaling against continuous CPAP pressure difficult, and it is prescribed for patients with central sleep apnea, obesity hypoventilation syndrome, or COPD overlap syndrome. BiPAP can also be used for non-invasive ventilation (NIV) in acute respiratory failure.

APAP (Auto-Adjusting CPAP / Auto-CPAP) — A CPAP device that automatically adjusts the delivered pressure breath-by-breath based on detected airway resistance, flow limitation, snoring, or apnea events. APAP delivers only as much pressure as needed at any given moment, resulting in a lower average pressure and potentially greater comfort compared to fixed-pressure CPAP. Most modern CPAP machines offer an auto mode.

IPAP / EPAP (Inspiratory / Expiratory Positive Airway Pressure) — The two pressure settings on a BiPAP device. IPAP is the higher pressure delivered during inhalation to support breathing and keep the airway open. EPAP is the lower pressure maintained during exhalation to prevent airway collapse. The difference between IPAP and EPAP (called "pressure support") aids ventilation. Typical settings range from 8–25 cmH2O for IPAP and 4–20 cmH2O for EPAP.

OSA (Obstructive Sleep Apnea) — A sleep disorder characterised by repeated collapse of the upper airway during sleep, causing breathing pauses (apneas) and oxygen desaturation. The airway obstruction is typically caused by relaxation of the throat muscles, excess tissue (in overweight individuals), or anatomical factors. OSA affects an estimated 7–13% of the adult Indian population and is associated with daytime sleepiness, hypertension, cardiovascular disease, and impaired cognitive function.

CSA (Central Sleep Apnea) — A sleep disorder in which the brain temporarily fails to send signals to the muscles that control breathing, resulting in breathing pauses without any physical airway obstruction. CSA is less common than OSA and is associated with heart failure, stroke, and opioid use. It requires different treatment approaches — often BiPAP with a backup rate or adaptive servo-ventilation (ASV) rather than standard CPAP.

Polysomnography (PSG) — A comprehensive sleep study conducted in a sleep laboratory that simultaneously records brain activity (EEG), eye movements (EOG), muscle activity (EMG), heart rhythm (ECG), breathing effort, airflow, oxygen saturation, and body position during a full night of sleep. PSG is the gold standard for diagnosing sleep disorders and for titrating CPAP/BiPAP pressures. Home sleep testing (HST) devices provide a simplified alternative for diagnosing uncomplicated OSA.

Ramp Pressure — A comfort feature on CPAP/BiPAP devices that starts the therapy at a low pressure and gradually increases to the prescribed therapeutic pressure over a set period (typically 5–45 minutes). This allows the patient to fall asleep at a comfortable, low pressure before the full therapeutic pressure is applied. Ramp is particularly helpful for patients who feel claustrophobic or uncomfortable at higher pressures.

Leak Rate — The volume of air escaping from the mask-face interface per unit time, typically measured in litres per minute (L/min). All masks have some intentional leak (through exhalation ports), but excessive unintentional leak reduces therapy effectiveness, causes eye irritation from air jets, increases noise, and triggers mask-off alarms. Modern CPAP devices compensate for moderate leaks automatically, but persistent high leak rates indicate poor mask fit.

Compliance — In sleep therapy, compliance refers to the patient's adherence to prescribed CPAP/BiPAP usage. The clinical standard for compliance is using the device for at least 4 hours per night on at least 70% of nights. Insurance reimbursement and treatment effectiveness both depend on compliance. Modern devices track usage data (hours used, AHI, leak rate) and can transmit this data wirelessly to the prescribing physician.

Wound Care and Pressure Relief Terms

Pressure Ulcer / Pressure Injury — Localised damage to the skin and underlying tissue, usually over a bony prominence (sacrum, heels, hips, elbows), resulting from sustained pressure or pressure combined with shear. Pressure injuries occur when blood flow to the tissue is reduced by external pressure exceeding capillary closing pressure, leading to ischaemia, cell death, and tissue necrosis. They are a major concern for bedridden, wheelchair-bound, and mobility-impaired patients.

Braden Scale — A widely used clinical tool for assessing a patient's risk of developing pressure ulcers. It scores six factors: sensory perception, moisture, activity, mobility, nutrition, and friction/shear. Each factor is scored on a scale, with a total score of 23 possible. A score of 18 or below indicates risk, with lower scores indicating higher risk. The Braden Scale guides healthcare providers in implementing preventive measures, including the use of pressure-relieving mattresses.

Capillary Closing Pressure — The external pressure required to collapse the capillary blood vessels in the skin and subcutaneous tissue, typically cited as 32 mmHg (though this varies by body site, age, and health status). When external pressure from a mattress, chair, or medical device exceeds capillary closing pressure for a sustained period, blood flow is occluded, and the tissue becomes ischaemic. Pressure-relieving surfaces aim to keep interface pressure below this threshold.

Shear Force — A mechanical force that acts parallel to the skin surface, occurring when the skin remains stationary (e.g., stuck to a bed sheet by friction) while the underlying tissue and bone slide in a different direction (e.g., when the patient slides down in bed). Shear distorts blood vessels and accelerates tissue damage, often acting in combination with pressure. Shear is a major contributing factor in sacral pressure injuries.

Alternating Pressure — A pressure redistribution technology used in anti-bedsore mattresses. The mattress contains multiple air cells arranged in rows that alternately inflate and deflate on a timed cycle (typically every 5–10 minutes). This ensures that no single area of the body bears continuous pressure, periodically relieving pressure on each contact point to restore blood flow. Alternating pressure mattresses are the most commonly prescribed active pressure relief surface for at-risk patients.

Static Immersion — A passive pressure redistribution approach in which the support surface (foam, gel, or air mattress) conforms to the patient's body contour, distributing weight over a larger surface area and reducing peak interface pressure at bony prominences. Unlike alternating pressure systems, static immersion surfaces do not require electricity or mechanical components. They are suitable for lower-risk patients or as a first-line preventive measure.

Pascal's Principle — A fundamental principle of fluid mechanics stating that pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally in all directions throughout the fluid. This principle underlies the design of air-cell and water-based pressure redistribution mattresses: when the patient's body compresses one area of the surface, the pressure is distributed evenly across the entire surface, reducing localised peak pressure at bony prominences.

NPUAP Staging (I–IV) — The classification system for pressure injuries developed by the National Pressure Ulcer Advisory Panel (now the National Pressure Injury Advisory Panel). Stage I: intact skin with non-blanchable redness. Stage II: partial-thickness skin loss (shallow open ulcer or blister). Stage III: full-thickness skin loss (fat may be visible, but bone/tendon/muscle are not exposed). Stage IV: full-thickness tissue loss with exposed bone, tendon, or muscle. Additional categories include "unstageable" (wound bed obscured by slough or eschar) and "deep tissue injury" (intact or non-intact skin with localised area of dark discolouration).

Wound Bed Preparation — A systematic approach to managing chronic wounds by addressing the barriers to healing. The framework is commonly summarised by the TIME acronym: Tissue management (debridement of non-viable tissue), Infection/inflammation control, Moisture balance (maintaining a moist but not macerated wound environment), and Edge advancement (evaluating whether the wound edges are migrating inward, indicating healing).

Debridement — The removal of dead (necrotic), damaged, or infected tissue from a wound to promote healing. Methods include sharp/surgical debridement (using a scalpel or scissors), autolytic debridement (using moisture-retentive dressings to allow the body's own enzymes to dissolve dead tissue), enzymatic debridement (applying topical enzymatic agents), and mechanical debridement (using wet-to-dry dressings or hydrotherapy). Debridement is a critical step in wound bed preparation.

NPWT (Negative Pressure Wound Therapy) — A wound treatment technique that applies controlled sub-atmospheric (negative) pressure to the wound bed through a sealed dressing connected to a vacuum pump. NPWT promotes healing by removing excess exudate, reducing oedema, increasing local blood flow, and mechanically stimulating tissue granulation. It is used for complex or non-healing wounds, including pressure ulcers, surgical wounds, and diabetic foot ulcers.

Medical Device Regulatory Terms

ISO 13485 — The international quality management system standard specifically designed for medical device organisations. It specifies requirements for design control, risk management, process validation, traceability, and corrective/preventive action. ISO 13485 certification is a regulatory prerequisite for market access in many countries and is increasingly expected by Indian hospitals and procurement committees.

ISO 9001 — The internationally recognised quality management system standard applicable to organisations in any industry. While widely held by medical device companies, ISO 9001 alone does not address medical device-specific requirements such as design controls, risk management, or regulatory compliance. It demonstrates general quality management competence but is not a substitute for ISO 13485 in the medical device context.

CE Mark — A mandatory marking for products sold within the European Economic Area, indicating conformity with applicable EU health, safety, and environmental regulations. For medical devices, CE marking is governed by the EU Medical Device Regulation (MDR 2017/745) and requires conformity assessment by a Notified Body for Class IIa devices and above. CE marking is also widely regarded as a quality signal in non-EU markets including India.

CDSCO (Central Drugs Standard Control Organisation) — India's national regulatory authority for pharmaceuticals and medical devices, operating under the Ministry of Health and Family Welfare. CDSCO oversees the registration and licensing of medical devices under the Medical Devices Rules, 2017, and is responsible for pre-market approval, post-market surveillance, and enforcement of device safety standards in India.

MDR 2017 (Medical Devices Rules, 2017) — India's dedicated regulatory framework for medical devices, established under the Drugs and Cosmetics Act. MDR 2017 classifies devices into four risk-based categories (A through D) and specifies registration and licensing requirements for each class. It represents a significant shift from the earlier approach of regulating only a handful of device categories under drug regulations.

NABCB (National Accreditation Board for Certification Bodies) — India's accreditation body for certification bodies, operating under the Quality Council of India. NABCB accredits the organisations that issue ISO certifications to manufacturers. An ISO certificate is only considered credible if the issuing certification body is accredited by NABCB or an equivalent international accreditation body (such as UKAS, DAkkS, or JAS-ANZ).

AQL (Acceptable Quality Level) — A statistical sampling methodology defined in ISO 2859 used in incoming and outgoing quality inspection. AQL specifies the maximum percentage of defective units that is considered acceptable in a production lot. For medical devices, AQL levels are typically set at 0.65% or 1.0% for critical defects and 2.5% for major defects. AQL inspection is used by manufacturers and procurement teams to accept or reject production batches.

CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action) — A formal quality management process required by ISO 13485. Corrective action addresses the root cause of an identified non-conformity or quality problem to prevent recurrence. Preventive action identifies potential problems before they occur and implements measures to prevent them. CAPA records are among the most scrutinised documents during regulatory audits and are a key indicator of a manufacturer's quality culture.

Design Control — A systematic approach to managing the design and development process for medical devices, required by ISO 13485 and most regulatory frameworks. Design control encompasses design planning, design inputs (requirements), design outputs (specifications), design reviews, design verification (does the output meet the input?), design validation (does the device meet user needs?), and design transfer (from development to manufacturing). Design control ensures that a device is designed correctly and meets its intended purpose.

Risk Management (ISO 14971) — The international standard for applying risk management to medical devices throughout their lifecycle. ISO 14971 defines a structured process for identifying hazards, estimating and evaluating associated risks, controlling risks to acceptable levels, and monitoring the effectiveness of risk controls. Risk management is integrated into every stage of device development and is a mandatory element of ISO 13485 and CE marking technical documentation.

GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice) — A set of principles and guidelines ensuring that medical devices (and pharmaceuticals) are consistently produced and controlled according to quality standards. In India, GMP requirements for medical devices are specified under MDR 2017 and cover premises, equipment, personnel, documentation, production controls, quality control, and complaint handling. GMP compliance is a prerequisite for obtaining a manufacturing license from CDSCO.

IEC 60601 (Electrical Safety for Medical Equipment) — The international standard for the safety and essential performance of medical electrical equipment, published by the International Electrotechnical Commission. IEC 60601-1 specifies general requirements, while collateral and particular standards (e.g., IEC 60601-1-2 for electromagnetic compatibility, IEC 80601-2-69 for oxygen concentrators) address specific aspects and device types. Compliance with IEC 60601 is a fundamental requirement for CE marking and is increasingly referenced in Indian medical device regulations.
