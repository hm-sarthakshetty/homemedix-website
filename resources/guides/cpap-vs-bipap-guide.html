CPAP vs. BiPAP: Understanding PAP Therapy for Sleep Apnea

Positive airway pressure (PAP) therapy is the gold standard treatment for sleep apnea, but the choice between CPAP and BiPAP depends on your specific diagnosis, pressure requirements, and comfort tolerance. This guide demystifies both therapies and helps you understand what to expect from treatment.

1. What Is Sleep Apnea?

Sleep apnea is a disorder characterised by repeated interruptions in breathing during sleep. These breathing pauses — called apneas (complete cessation of airflow for at least 10 seconds) or hypopneas (partial reduction in airflow with associated oxygen desaturation or arousal) — can occur dozens to hundreds of times per night, fragmenting sleep architecture and causing intermittent hypoxemia that stresses the cardiovascular system.

Types of Sleep Apnea

  • Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA): The most common type, accounting for approximately 85% of cases. During sleep, the muscles of the upper airway relax, allowing the tongue and soft palate to collapse backward and obstruct the pharyngeal airway. The brain detects the resulting oxygen drop and triggers a brief arousal (often subconscious) to restore muscle tone and reopen the airway. The patient typically resumes breathing with a loud snort or gasp, then falls back asleep — only for the cycle to repeat. Risk factors include obesity (particularly neck circumference above 43 cm in men, 38 cm in women), male sex, age over 40, craniofacial anatomy (retrognathia, macroglossia), and nasal obstruction.
  • Central Sleep Apnea (CSA): Less common, accounting for about 5–10% of cases. The airway is not physically obstructed; rather, the brain's respiratory control centre in the medulla fails to send appropriate signals to the muscles of respiration. The result is a temporary cessation of both airflow and respiratory effort. CSA is associated with congestive heart failure (Cheyne-Stokes respiration), stroke, brainstem lesions, and high-altitude exposure. It can also emerge as "complex sleep apnea" in patients initially treated for OSA with CPAP.
  • Mixed Sleep Apnea: A combination of obstructive and central events within the same sleep study. Each apnea episode begins as a central event (no effort) and transitions to an obstructive event (effort against a closed airway) as the brain attempts to resume breathing.

Prevalence in India

Sleep apnea is vastly underdiagnosed in India. Population-based studies estimate that OSA of at least moderate severity (Apnea-Hypopnea Index, or AHI, of 15 or more events per hour) affects 7–14% of Indian men and 3–5% of Indian women aged 30–60 years. With India's population, this translates to an estimated 30–40 million people with clinically significant sleep apnea — yet fewer than 5% have been diagnosed, and fewer than 2% are on treatment. This "diagnosis gap" is driven by low awareness among the public and even among many general practitioners, limited access to sleep laboratories for polysomnography (the gold standard diagnostic test), cultural normalisation of snoring, and the perception that daytime sleepiness is simply due to work stress or lifestyle.

Consequences of Untreated Sleep Apnea

Sleep apnea is not merely a quality-of-life issue. Untreated moderate-to-severe OSA carries serious, well-documented health consequences:

  • Hypertension: OSA is an independent risk factor for systemic hypertension. The cyclical hypoxemia and sympathetic nervous system activation during apneas cause sustained daytime blood pressure elevation. Approximately 50% of OSA patients have hypertension, and 30% of hypertensive patients have undiagnosed OSA.
  • Cardiovascular disease: Untreated severe OSA increases the risk of atrial fibrillation (4x), stroke (3x), and myocardial infarction (2–3x). The Wisconsin Sleep Cohort Study showed a 5.2-fold increase in cardiovascular mortality in untreated severe OSA over 18 years.
  • Type 2 diabetes: OSA independently worsens insulin resistance and glycaemic control, creating a bidirectional relationship with metabolic syndrome.
  • Motor vehicle accidents: Excessive daytime sleepiness from fragmented sleep increases accident risk by 2–7 times. This is particularly dangerous for commercial drivers, a significant population in India.
  • Cognitive decline: Chronic intermittent hypoxemia and sleep fragmentation impair memory consolidation, executive function, and mood regulation, contributing to depression, anxiety, and accelerated cognitive decline.

2. How CPAP Works

Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP) is the first-line treatment for moderate-to-severe obstructive sleep apnea. Introduced by Professor Colin Sullivan in 1981, CPAP & BiPAP devices have accumulated over four decades of clinical evidence demonstrating their efficacy in eliminating apneas, improving oxygenation, reducing cardiovascular risk, and restoring quality of life.

The Mechanism

A CPAP machine is essentially a precision air blower. It draws in room air through a filtered intake, pressurises it using a motor-driven turbine or blower, and delivers it through a heated humidifier chamber and flexible tubing to a mask worn over the patient's nose (or nose and mouth). The pressurised air acts as a "pneumatic splint" — the continuous positive pressure (measured in centimetres of water, or cmH2O) holds the soft tissues of the upper airway open, preventing the collapse that causes obstructive apneas and hypopneas.

The prescribed pressure is determined during a sleep study (either an in-laboratory titration polysomnography or a home sleep test followed by auto-titration). Typical therapeutic pressures range from 5 to 20 cmH2O, with most patients requiring 8–12 cmH2O. The pressure is fixed and continuous throughout both inspiration and expiration — the same pressure is delivered whether the patient is breathing in or breathing out.

Auto-CPAP (APAP)

Auto-titrating CPAP (APAP) is a more sophisticated variant that continuously monitors airflow and adjusts the delivered pressure breath-by-breath within a physician-set range (e.g., 5–15 cmH2O). When the algorithm detects flow limitation, snoring vibration, or an incipient apnea, it automatically increases pressure to abort the event. When breathing is stable, pressure decreases to the minimum effective level. APAP offers several advantages: it accommodates pressure needs that change with sleep position (supine vs. lateral), sleep stage (REM sleep requires higher pressure), nasal congestion, alcohol consumption, and body weight changes. Many patients find APAP more comfortable than fixed CPAP because the average delivered pressure is lower.

When Is CPAP Prescribed?

  • Moderate OSA (AHI 15–30/hr) with symptoms (daytime sleepiness, unrefreshing sleep, morning headaches)
  • Severe OSA (AHI > 30/hr) — regardless of symptoms, due to cardiovascular risk
  • Mild OSA (AHI 5–15/hr) when accompanied by significant symptoms, cardiovascular comorbidities, or occupational risk (e.g., commercial drivers)

3. How BiPAP Works

Bilevel Positive Airway Pressure (BiPAP, also written as BPAP) delivers two distinct pressure levels: a higher pressure during inspiration (IPAP — Inspiratory Positive Airway Pressure) and a lower pressure during expiration (EPAP — Expiratory Positive Airway Pressure). The machine detects the transition between inspiration and expiration by monitoring airflow and pressure changes, and rapidly switches between the two pressure settings.

The Mechanism

Like CPAP, BiPAP splints the upper airway open. However, the bilevel design serves additional physiological purposes. The higher IPAP actively assists inspiration by augmenting the patient's tidal volume — essentially providing partial ventilatory support. The lower EPAP maintains airway patency during expiration (preventing apneas) while making it easier for the patient to breathe out against the mask pressure. The difference between IPAP and EPAP (called "pressure support") determines the degree of ventilatory assistance: a pressure support of 4–6 cmH2O provides mild assistance, while 10–15 cmH2O provides substantial ventilatory support approaching non-invasive ventilation.

Typical BiPAP settings might be: IPAP 16 cmH2O / EPAP 10 cmH2O (pressure support of 6), or IPAP 22 cmH2O / EPAP 8 cmH2O (pressure support of 14). More advanced BiPAP modes include:

  • Spontaneous (S) mode: The machine cycles between IPAP and EPAP in response to the patient's own breathing effort. This is the standard mode for most patients.
  • Spontaneous/Timed (S/T) mode: A backup respiratory rate is set (e.g., 12 breaths per minute). If the patient fails to initiate a breath within the timed interval, the machine delivers a mandatory breath at IPAP. This is critical for patients with central apneas who may have prolonged pauses in respiratory drive.
  • Timed (T) mode: The machine delivers all breaths at a fixed rate, regardless of the patient's effort. Used for patients with no reliable spontaneous breathing drive.

When Is BiPAP Prescribed?

  • Central sleep apnea: CPAP does not address central apneas (where the problem is absent respiratory drive, not airway obstruction). BiPAP in S/T mode provides the backup ventilation that central apnea patients need.
  • Obesity hypoventilation syndrome (OHS): Patients with BMI > 30 and chronic daytime hypercapnia (elevated CO2) need the ventilatory support that BiPAP's pressure differential provides to augment tidal volume and reduce CO2.
  • COPD-OSA overlap syndrome: Patients who have both COPD and OSA benefit from BiPAP because the higher IPAP supports inspiration (compensating for COPD-related air trapping and respiratory muscle fatigue) while the lower EPAP reduces the work of exhaling against pressure.
  • Neuromuscular conditions: Patients with motor neurone disease, muscular dystrophy, or other conditions that weaken respiratory muscles use BiPAP as non-invasive ventilation (NIV) to support breathing during sleep and, as the disease progresses, during daytime as well.
  • CPAP intolerance: Some patients cannot tolerate the fixed pressure of CPAP, particularly at higher settings (above 15 cmH2O). They report difficulty exhaling against the continuous pressure ("like breathing out through a straw"). BiPAP alleviates this by dropping to a lower EPAP during expiration, reducing the sensation of resistance.

4. Decision Guide: CPAP vs. BiPAP

The decision between CPAP and BiPAP is a medical one made by your sleep physician based on the type of sleep apnea, coexisting conditions, and pressure tolerance. However, understanding the rationale helps patients engage as informed participants in their treatment plan.

CPAP vs. BiPAP: When to Use Which
Parameter CPAP BiPAP
Pressure Delivery Single fixed pressure (or auto-adjusting range) Two pressures: higher IPAP on inhale, lower EPAP on exhale
Primary Indication Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) Central apnea, OHS, COPD overlap, neuromuscular, CPAP-intolerant
Ventilatory Support None (splints airway only) Yes (IPAP-EPAP differential augments tidal volume)
Typical Pressure Range 4–20 cmH2O IPAP: 8–30 cmH2O; EPAP: 4–20 cmH2O
Comfort (Exhaling) Some patients find exhaling against fixed pressure uncomfortable at higher settings Easier exhalation due to lower EPAP
Backup Rate No (patient must breathe spontaneously) Available in S/T and T modes (delivers breaths if patient pauses)
Complexity Simpler setup, fewer settings to adjust More complex, requires sleep physician titration
Cost (India) Rs 25,000–80,000 (fixed); Rs 40,000–1,20,000 (auto) Rs 60,000–2,00,000
Insurance Coverage Covered under most health insurance plans with sleep study documentation Covered, but may require additional clinical justification

The general rule: Start with CPAP for uncomplicated OSA. If CPAP fails (persistent central apneas, intolerable pressure, or coexisting conditions requiring ventilatory support), step up to BiPAP. Your sleep physician will make this determination based on your titration study data, treatment response, and clinical picture. Never switch from CPAP to BiPAP (or vice versa) without medical guidance, as incorrect settings can worsen sleep-disordered breathing.

5. Living with PAP Therapy

The most effective PAP therapy machine in the world is useless if the patient does not wear it. Adherence to PAP therapy is the single greatest challenge in sleep apnea treatment, with studies reporting that 30–50% of patients abandon therapy within the first year. Understanding what to expect and how to optimise comfort can dramatically improve your chances of long-term success.

Mask Selection

The mask is the most critical interface between the patient and the machine. A poorly fitting mask causes air leaks, skin irritation, noise, and frustration — the top reasons patients give up on PAP therapy. There are three main mask types:

  • Nasal mask: Covers only the nose. The most popular type, offering a good balance of seal, comfort, and field of vision. Ideal for patients who breathe through the nose and keep their mouth closed during sleep. Available in a wide range of sizes.
  • Nasal pillows: Small silicone inserts that sit inside the nostrils. The least intrusive option — no forehead frame, no bridge-of-nose pressure. Best for patients who feel claustrophobic with larger masks, who wear glasses in bed, or who move frequently during sleep. Not suitable for high pressures (above 15 cmH2O), as the pillows may dislodge.
  • Full-face mask: Covers both the nose and mouth. Necessary for patients who breathe through the mouth during sleep (mouth breathers), who have chronic nasal obstruction, or who require high pressures. Larger and heavier than nasal masks, with a steeper learning curve, but modern full-face designs have become significantly lighter and less claustrophobic than earlier generations.

Most sleep labs and equipment suppliers will let you try multiple mask styles before purchasing. Take advantage of this — the right mask makes the difference between a patient who uses their machine every night and one who relegates it to the closet.

The First 30 Days: Adaptation Tips

The first month of PAP therapy is the critical window that determines long-term adherence. Here are evidence-based strategies for a successful adaptation:

  1. Start slow: Wear the mask for 30 minutes while watching television or reading before attempting to sleep with it. Gradually increase wearing time over the first week until you can fall asleep with it.
  2. Use the "ramp" feature: Most CPAP and BiPAP machines have a ramp function that starts pressure at a very low level (4 cmH2O) and gradually increases to the prescribed pressure over 15–45 minutes. This allows you to fall asleep at a comfortable low pressure and reach therapeutic pressure after you are asleep.
  3. Address nasal congestion proactively: Dry or congested nasal passages are the most common comfort complaint. Use a saline nasal spray before bed, and ensure the machine's heated humidifier is turned on and set to an appropriate level. In dry Indian winters or air-conditioned rooms, you may need a higher humidity setting.
  4. Keep a sleep diary: Record your usage hours, how you felt in the morning, any mask issues, and any symptoms. Share this with your sleep physician at your follow-up appointment (typically at 2 weeks and 4 weeks after starting therapy).
  5. Expect improvement in 1–2 weeks: Many patients report dramatically improved daytime alertness, mood, and energy within the first 7–14 days of consistent use. This positive reinforcement is the strongest motivator for continued adherence.

Humidification in the Indian Climate

India's climate varies enormously, from the arid deserts of Rajasthan to the humid coasts of Kerala. Humidification needs vary accordingly. In humid climates, excessive humidifier settings can cause "rainout" — condensation accumulating in the tubing and splashing the face. Reduce the humidifier setting or use a heated tube (which maintains air temperature throughout the tubing length, preventing condensation). In dry climates or air-conditioned bedrooms, increase the humidifier setting to prevent nasal dryness, nosebleeds, and throat irritation.

Travelling with PAP

Modern PAP machines are designed for travel: most weigh 1–2 kg, accept universal voltage (100–240V AC), and are approved for airline carry-on. When travelling in India, carry a letter from your sleep physician confirming the medical necessity of the device — this is helpful at airport security and if questioned by train authorities. For camping or areas without electricity, battery packs are available that provide 1–2 nights of use per charge.

Cleaning Schedule

  • Daily: Wipe the mask cushion with a damp cloth or unscented baby wipe. Empty and air-dry the humidifier chamber.
  • Weekly: Wash the mask, headgear, and tubing in warm water with mild soap. Rinse thoroughly and air-dry away from direct sunlight. Clean the humidifier chamber with white vinegar solution (1:3 vinegar to water) to prevent mineral buildup.
  • Monthly: Inspect the mask cushion for wear, cracks, or loss of seal. Check tubing for holes or discolouration. Clean or replace the machine's air intake filter (foam filter: wash and dry; fine filter: replace).
  • Every 3–6 months: Replace the mask cushion (silicone degrades and loses seal quality over time). Replace tubing if it becomes stiff, discoloured, or develops pinholes.

Compliance Tracking

Modern PAP machines record detailed usage data: hours of use per night, mask leak rates, residual AHI (how many apneas remain despite treatment), and pressure delivery patterns. Many machines transmit this data wirelessly to a cloud platform that your sleep physician can access remotely. In India, insurance companies increasingly require documented compliance (typically defined as at least 4 hours of use on at least 70% of nights over a 30-day period) for continued coverage. Even without insurance requirements, reviewing your compliance data with your doctor helps optimise therapy settings and troubleshoot issues early.
