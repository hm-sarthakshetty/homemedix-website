Pressure Ulcer Staging and Treatment: A Clinical Reference for Indian Healthcare

Pressure injuries remain one of the most common yet preventable complications of prolonged immobility. In Indian hospitals, ICU-based incidence rates range from 4% to 18%, with home-care patients facing even higher risks due to limited nursing support. This guide provides a clinically grounded overview of staging, risk assessment, evidence-based treatment, and the critical role of support surfaces in pressure ulcer management.

Understanding Pressure Injuries

A pressure injury (formerly called pressure ulcer, decubitus ulcer, or bedsore) is localised damage to the skin and underlying soft tissue, usually over a bony prominence or related to a medical device. The damage occurs as a result of intense and/or prolonged pressure, or pressure in combination with shear forces.

The Pathophysiology of Tissue Damage

Normal capillary closing pressure — the pressure required to compress capillaries and stop blood flow — is approximately 32 mmHg (though this varies with patient factors). When external pressure on the skin exceeds this threshold for a sustained period, blood flow to the tissue is occluded. The resulting ischaemia leads to a cascade of damage:

  1. Ischaemia: Tissue is deprived of oxygen and nutrients within 1–2 hours of sustained pressure
  2. Reperfusion injury: When pressure is briefly relieved, returning blood flow generates reactive oxygen species that further damage cells
  3. Cell death and necrosis: If ischaemia continues, irreversible tissue destruction begins — starting at the muscle layer (which is most metabolically active and therefore most susceptible) and progressing outward to the skin
  4. Wound formation: The visible skin wound is often only the "tip of the iceberg," with more extensive damage in deeper tissues

Contributing Factors Beyond Pressure

  • Shear: When the skeleton slides relative to the skin (e.g., when a patient slides down a raised bed), blood vessels are stretched and kinked, amplifying ischaemic damage. Shear forces are considered more destructive than pressure alone.
  • Friction: Surface rubbing against skin (during repositioning) strips the epidermis and makes the tissue more vulnerable to pressure damage.
  • Moisture: Prolonged exposure to perspiration, urine, or faecal matter macerates the skin, weakening its barrier function and increasing friction coefficient by up to 5-fold.
  • Malnutrition: Albumin levels below 3.5 g/dL significantly impair wound healing. Protein, zinc, vitamin C, and caloric intake are critical for tissue repair.

High-Risk Populations

In the Indian context, pressure injuries most commonly affect:

  • Spinal cord injury patients (lifetime risk estimated at 25–80%)
  • Elderly patients immobilised after hip fracture or stroke
  • ICU patients on prolonged mechanical ventilation
  • Patients with advanced cancer, particularly those on palliative care at home
  • Individuals with diabetes and peripheral neuropathy who cannot sense early warning pain

NPUAP/EPUAP Classification

The internationally accepted classification system from the National Pressure Injury Advisory Panel (NPIAP, formerly NPUAP) and European Pressure Ulcer Advisory Panel (EPUAP) defines six categories. Accurate staging is essential for appropriate treatment selection.

Stage 1: Non-Blanchable Erythema

Intact skin with a localised area of non-blanchable erythema — when you press a finger on the reddened area and release, it does not turn white (blanch) and then re-redden. In darkly pigmented skin (common in Indian patients), blanching may be difficult to detect; instead look for colour differences compared to surrounding skin, changes in skin temperature (warmth), tissue consistency (boggy feel), or pain/itching at the site. The area may appear purple or maroon. Stage 1 injuries are fully reversible with prompt pressure offloading.

Stage 2: Partial-Thickness Skin Loss

The dermis is partially exposed, presenting as a shallow open ulcer with a pink-red wound bed. May also present as an intact or ruptured serum-filled blister. There is no slough or eschar (dead tissue) visible, and subcutaneous fat is not exposed. This stage should not be used to describe moisture-associated skin damage, tape burns, or skin tears — these have different causes and treatment pathways.

Stage 3: Full-Thickness Skin Loss

The full thickness of the skin is destroyed. Subcutaneous fat may be visible, and slough (yellow-tan dead tissue) may be present but does not obscure the depth of the wound. Bone, tendon, and muscle are not directly visible or palpable. The depth of a Stage 3 injury varies by anatomical location — it may be shallow on the bridge of the nose (minimal subcutaneous tissue) or very deep on the buttock or heel. Undermining and tunnelling may be present.

Stage 4: Full-Thickness Tissue Loss

The most severe stage, involving full-thickness tissue loss with exposed bone, tendon, muscle, or cartilage directly visible or palpable in the wound. Slough or eschar may be present on some parts of the wound bed. Osteomyelitis (bone infection) is a common complication at this stage. Undermining, tunnelling, and sinus tracts are frequently observed. Stage 4 injuries often require surgical intervention and carry significant morbidity and mortality risk.

Unstageable

Full-thickness tissue loss in which the base of the wound is covered by slough (yellow, tan, gray, green, or brown) and/or eschar (tan, brown, or black hard necrotic tissue) in the wound bed. Until sufficient slough or eschar is removed to expose the wound base, the true depth and therefore the true stage cannot be determined. Stable eschar on the heel serves as the body's natural biological cover and should generally not be removed unless signs of infection develop.

Deep Tissue Pressure Injury (DTPI)

Intact or non-intact skin with a localised area of persistent non-blanchable deep red, maroon, or purple discolouration, or epidermal separation revealing a dark wound bed or blood-filled blister. This represents damage to underlying soft tissue from pressure and/or shear. The wound may evolve rapidly to reveal the actual extent of tissue injury, or may resolve without tissue loss. DTPI is particularly important to identify early, as the surface appearance may drastically underrepresent the severity of deep tissue damage.

Risk Assessment: The Braden Scale

The Braden Scale is the most widely validated and used risk assessment tool for pressure injury prevention worldwide. It provides a structured, objective method for identifying patients at risk before a wound develops, enabling targeted preventive interventions.

The Six Subscales

Each subscale is scored from 1 (worst) to 3 or 4 (best), for a total possible score of 6–23:

Score Interpretation

  • 19–23: No significant risk — standard preventive measures
  • 15–18: Mild risk — implement turning schedule, inspect skin daily, optimise nutrition
  • 13–14: Moderate risk — add pressure redistribution surface (foam overlay or alternating pressure), increase repositioning frequency
  • 10–12: High risk — active pressure redistribution surface (alternating pressure mattress), aggressive nutritional support, moisture management protocol
  • < 10: Very high risk — advanced pressure redistribution (low-air-loss or water immersion surface), q1–2h repositioning, full multidisciplinary wound care team involvement

The Braden Scale should be completed on admission to any healthcare setting (including home care initiation), then reassessed regularly — daily in acute care, weekly in long-term care, and at every home nursing visit.

Treatment by Stage

Treatment of pressure injuries follows the fundamental principle of addressing the cause (offloading pressure) while optimising the wound healing environment. The approach varies significantly by stage.

Stage 1–2: Conservative Management

  • Pressure offloading: Reposition the patient at least every 2 hours (q2h). Use the 30-degree lateral tilt position to avoid direct pressure on the trochanter and sacrum. Elevate heels completely off the bed surface using pillows or heel suspension devices.
  • Moisture barriers: Apply dimethicone-based barrier cream to protect intact skin from incontinence-related maceration.
  • Appropriate dressings: Stage 1 typically requires only a thin hydrocolloid or transparent film dressing for protection. Stage 2 wounds benefit from hydrocolloid dressings (e.g., DuoDERM) or foam dressings that maintain a moist wound healing environment while managing exudate.
  • Pressure redistribution surfaces: At minimum, a high-density foam overlay. For patients who cannot be reliably repositioned, an alternating pressure mattress such as the HomeMedix AirQuilt is indicated — these systems cyclically inflate and deflate air cells to periodically relieve pressure from different body areas.
  • Nutrition: Ensure protein intake of at least 1.25–1.5 g/kg/day. Supplement with vitamin C (250 mg twice daily) and zinc (220 mg daily) if deficiency is suspected.

Stage 3–4: Advanced Wound Management

  • Wound bed preparation: Follow the TIME framework — manage Tissue (debride non-viable tissue), control Infection/Inflammation, maintain Moisture balance, and assess Edges (are they advancing?)
  • Debridement: Remove necrotic tissue to allow healing. Methods include sharp/surgical debridement (fastest, performed by a trained clinician), autolytic debridement (using moisture-retentive dressings to soften eschar), enzymatic debridement (collagenase-based ointments), and mechanical debridement (wet-to-dry gauze, though this is now discouraged due to non-selective tissue removal).
  • Infection management: If the wound shows signs of local infection (increased pain, warmth, erythema, purulent discharge, foul odour), obtain wound cultures (preferably tissue biopsy, not swab) and initiate topical antimicrobials (silver-based dressings, cadexomer iodine). Systemic antibiotics are reserved for cellulitis, osteomyelitis, or sepsis.
  • Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT): Also known as VAC therapy, this applies controlled sub-atmospheric pressure to the wound bed via a sealed foam dressing and pump. NPWT promotes granulation tissue formation, reduces oedema, and removes exudate. It is increasingly available in Indian tertiary centres and for home use with portable devices.
  • Support surfaces: Stage 3–4 injuries require advanced support surfaces — static immersion mattresses (water-filled, gel, or viscoelastic foam) or powered surfaces (alternating pressure, low-air-loss) that achieve higher levels of pressure redistribution than standard foam.
  • Surgical intervention: Stage 4 ulcers that fail conservative management may require surgical closure with myocutaneous flap procedures, performed by a plastic surgeon. Post-operative care requires specialised pressure redistribution and strict immobilisation protocols.

Support Surfaces: Choosing the Right One

Support surface selection is one of the most impactful decisions in pressure injury prevention and treatment. The right surface can mean the difference between healing and deterioration. Understanding the categories and evidence behind each type is essential.

Reactive (Constant Low Pressure) Surfaces

These surfaces respond to body weight and position by conforming to the patient's shape, thereby distributing pressure over a larger area and reducing peak interface pressure:

  • High-density foam: The baseline for at-risk patients. Should be at least 15 cm thick with a density of 35–40 kg/m³. Standard hospital mattresses in India often do not meet this specification — upgrading is the first step in prevention.
  • Gel overlays: Provide excellent pressure distribution and temperature management. Heavier and more expensive than foam, but do not bottom out as readily.
  • Water-filled surfaces: Based on Pascal's principle — pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is distributed equally in all directions. A properly designed medical water mattress like the HomeMedix AquaQuilt can achieve interface pressures below capillary closing pressure across most bony prominences. Ideal for patients who cannot be regularly repositioned.

Active (Alternating Pressure) Surfaces

These surfaces use a powered pump to cyclically inflate and deflate individual air cells, typically in 5–10 minute alternating cycles. During the deflation phase, tissue over those cells experiences near-zero pressure, allowing reperfusion. Key specifications to evaluate:

  • Cell height: Minimum 10 cm when inflated for adequate immersion depth. Taller cells (12.5–15 cm) provide better pressure redistribution for heavier patients.
  • Cycle time: Typically 5–10 minutes per phase. Shorter cycles may not allow adequate reperfusion; longer cycles may allow ischaemic damage during the inflation phase.
  • Number of cells: More cells allow finer-grained pressure variation. Systems with 16–20 cells across the mattress length are standard.
  • Static mode: The pump should offer a static (non-alternating) mode for patient comfort during meals, procedures, or at the patient's preference.
  • Noise level: A critical consideration for home use — pumps operating above 40 dB can disturb sleep. The quietest systems operate at 30–35 dB.

Practical Considerations for Indian Home Care

When selecting a support surface for home use in India, consider these practical factors:

  • Power reliability: Powered alternating pressure systems stop functioning during power outages, causing the patient to "bottom out" on a deflated mattress. Ensure a battery backup or inverter is available, or choose a surface that maintains function without power (water, high-density foam).
  • Climate: India's hot climate makes breathability important. Impermeable surfaces cause excessive sweating and maceration. Look for covers with moisture-wicking and vapour-permeable properties.
  • Caregiver capability: Some advanced surfaces require regular maintenance (water level checks, pump filter cleaning). Match the surface complexity to the caregiver's ability and training.
  • Cost-effectiveness: While advanced powered surfaces have higher upfront costs, they are vastly more cost-effective than treating a Stage 3–4 pressure ulcer, which can cost Rs 2–5 lakh or more in hospitalisation, surgery, and wound care supplies.
