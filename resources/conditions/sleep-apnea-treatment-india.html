Sleep Apnea in India: Diagnosis, Treatment, and Living with PAP Therapy

Obstructive sleep apnea affects an estimated 13–14% of urban Indian adults, yet awareness remains critically low. Left untreated, it silently raises the risk of hypertension, heart disease, stroke, and road accidents. The good news: with proper diagnosis and nightly PAP therapy, most patients experience a transformative improvement in energy, focus, and long-term health outcomes.

What Is Sleep Apnea?

Sleep apnea is a disorder in which breathing repeatedly stops and starts during sleep. There are three distinct types:

  • Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA): The most common form, accounting for roughly 84% of cases. During sleep, the muscles of the upper airway relax excessively, causing the soft palate, tongue base, and pharyngeal walls to collapse inward. This creates a partial obstruction (hypopnea) or complete blockage (apnea) lasting at least 10 seconds, often much longer. The brain detects dropping oxygen levels and triggers a micro-arousal to restore muscle tone — the patient briefly wakes (often without awareness), gasps, and resumes breathing.
  • Central Sleep Apnea (CSA): The airway remains open, but the brain fails to send proper signals to the respiratory muscles. CSA is less common, typically seen in patients with heart failure, stroke, or opioid use.
  • Mixed/Complex Sleep Apnea: Features of both obstructive and central apnea, sometimes emerging when OSA patients are started on CPAP therapy.

Prevalence in India

Studies conducted at the All India Institute of Medical Sciences (AIIMS), New Delhi, and other tertiary centres have reported OSA prevalence of 13.7% in urban adult men and 5.6% in women, with rates climbing sharply in populations over 40 years of age. These figures rival or exceed those reported in Western countries, yet awareness among Indian physicians — particularly in primary care — remains limited.

Risk Factors Specific to the Indian Population

  • Obesity and central adiposity: While overall BMI may be lower than Western populations, Indians tend to carry more visceral and neck fat at lower BMIs ("thin-fat" phenotype). A neck circumference > 40 cm in men or > 36 cm in women is a significant risk marker.
  • Craniofacial anatomy: Studies suggest that South Asian craniofacial morphology — including smaller mandibular dimensions and a more posteriorly positioned maxilla — may predispose to airway narrowing during sleep, even in non-obese individuals.
  • Male sex: Men are 2–3 times more likely to develop OSA than premenopausal women, though the gap narrows after menopause.
  • Age: Prevalence increases progressively after age 40 and peaks between 50–70 years.
  • Alcohol and sedative use: These further relax upper airway muscles and depress the arousal response.

Symptoms and Health Consequences

Sleep apnea is often called a "silent epidemic" because patients may be entirely unaware of their nocturnal breathing disturbances. The most common symptoms include:

  • Loud, habitual snoring: Often the first symptom noticed — typically by a bed partner. The snoring is usually loudest when sleeping on the back (supine) and may include audible pauses followed by choking or gasping sounds.
  • Witnessed apneas: A partner observes the patient stop breathing, sometimes for alarmingly long periods (30–60 seconds or more).
  • Excessive daytime sleepiness (EDS): Measured clinically using the Epworth Sleepiness Scale (ESS), a self-reported questionnaire scored from 0–24. A score above 10 suggests abnormal sleepiness; above 16 indicates severe sleepiness. Patients may doze off during meetings, while watching television, or dangerously, while driving.
  • Morning headaches: Caused by nocturnal CO2 retention and cerebral vasodilation.
  • Nocturia: Frequent nighttime urination, driven by increased atrial natriuretic peptide secretion from pressure changes in the thorax.
  • Mood disturbances: Irritability, depression, and difficulty concentrating are common and frequently misattributed to other causes.

Getting Diagnosed in India

If you or a family member suspect sleep apnea, the diagnostic pathway in India typically involves the following steps:

Level 1: In-Lab Polysomnography (PSG)

The gold standard for sleep apnea diagnosis is overnight attended polysomnography conducted in a sleep laboratory. This comprehensive test simultaneously records brain waves (EEG), eye movements (EOG), chin and leg muscle activity (EMG), heart rhythm (ECG), nasal airflow, respiratory effort (chest and abdominal belts), and pulse oximetry. A trained sleep technologist monitors the study in real-time.

Sleep labs are available in most Tier 1 cities — AIIMS, Medanta, Apollo, Fortis, and NIMHANS all operate accredited sleep centres. Costs typically range from Rs 8,000 to Rs 20,000 depending on the facility.

Level 3: Home Sleep Testing (HST)

For patients with a high pre-test probability of moderate-to-severe OSA and no significant comorbidities, a Level 3 portable home sleep test may be sufficient. These devices record nasal airflow, respiratory effort, pulse oximetry, and body position — but not brain waves, so they cannot differentiate sleep stages. HSTs are less expensive (Rs 3,000–8,000) and more convenient but may underestimate severity in mild cases.

Understanding Your AHI Score

The key metric from any sleep study is the Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI) — the number of apneas and hypopneas per hour of sleep:

PAP Therapy: Your Primary Treatment

Positive Airway Pressure (PAP) therapy is the first-line treatment for moderate-to-severe OSA. The device delivers pressurised air through a mask worn during sleep, acting as a pneumatic splint to keep the upper airway open and prevent collapse.

CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure)

CPAP delivers a fixed, continuous pressure throughout the breathing cycle. It is the standard treatment for uncomplicated obstructive sleep apnea. The prescribed pressure (typically 6–14 cmH2O) is determined during a titration study or by using an auto-titrating machine for a trial period. CPAP is effective for the vast majority of OSA patients.

BiPAP (Bilevel Positive Airway Pressure)

BiPAP delivers two distinct pressures: a higher inspiratory pressure (IPAP) and a lower expiratory pressure (EPAP). The pressure drop during exhalation makes breathing more comfortable, particularly at higher therapeutic pressures. BiPAP is indicated for:

  • Patients who cannot tolerate high CPAP pressures (> 15 cmH2O)
  • Central or complex sleep apnea
  • Obesity Hypoventilation Syndrome (OHS) where ventilatory support is needed
  • Patients with concurrent COPD or neuromuscular disease

Auto-Titrating (APAP) Machines

Auto-PAP machines continuously monitor airflow and adjust pressure breath-by-breath within a set range. They deliver lower average pressures than fixed CPAP, which many patients find more comfortable. APAP is increasingly used both for initial home titration and as a long-term therapy option.

Choosing the Right Mask

Mask selection is arguably as important as the machine itself. An ill-fitting mask causes leaks, skin irritation, and poor compliance. The three main types are:

  • Nasal mask: Covers only the nose. The most popular choice — lightweight, less claustrophobic, effective for most patients. Requires mouth to stay closed (chin straps may help).
  • Nasal pillows: Small cushions that seal at the nostrils. Minimal facial contact, ideal for patients who feel claustrophobic. Best for pressures below 12 cmH2O.
  • Full-face mask: Covers nose and mouth. Required for mouth breathers or patients needing higher pressures. Larger profile but eliminates mouth leak issues.

Humidification in the Indian Climate

Most modern PAP machines include a heated humidifier. In India's hot and humid climate, you may need less humidification than patients in temperate countries. However, air-conditioned bedrooms can be quite dry, making humidification essential. Start at a moderate setting and adjust based on comfort — too much humidity causes "rainout" (condensation in the tubing), while too little causes nasal dryness and congestion.

Adapting to PAP: The First 30 Days and Beyond

Starting PAP therapy is a significant lifestyle adjustment. Studies consistently show that long-term compliance is largely determined by the experience during the first 1–4 weeks. Here is a structured approach to maximising your chances of success:

Week 1: Acclimatisation

  • Wear the mask while awake for 20–30 minutes daily (while reading or watching television) to get accustomed to the sensation.
  • Use the ramp feature: Most machines offer a ramp mode that starts at a very low pressure (4 cmH2O) and gradually increases to the therapeutic pressure over 15–45 minutes, helping you fall asleep before full pressure is reached.
  • Expect some discomfort: Mild nasal congestion, dry mouth, and a sense of air pressure are normal in the first few nights. These typically improve as your body adapts.

Weeks 2–4: Building the Habit

  • Aim for at least 4 hours per night initially, then gradually increase to full-night use. The clinical threshold for "compliance" is typically defined as 4 hours per night for 70% of nights, but maximum benefit comes from wearing the device for the entire sleep period.
  • Address mask leak: If you wake up frequently or notice marks on your face, the mask fit needs adjustment. Tighter is not always better — over-tightening causes more leaks, not fewer. Refit the mask while lying in your sleeping position.
  • Manage nasal congestion: Use saline nasal spray before bed. If congestion persists, your doctor may prescribe a nasal corticosteroid spray (fluticasone or mometasone). The heated humidifier should be turned on.

Long-Term Success Strategies

  • Establish a consistent sleep routine: Go to bed and wake up at the same time daily. Put on the mask as the very first step of getting into bed.
  • Review data regularly: Modern PAP machines record usage hours, AHI, and leak data. Many sync to smartphone apps. Review this data at follow-up appointments (at 1 month, then every 3–6 months).
  • Replace supplies on schedule: Mask cushions lose their seal over time (replace every 1–3 months), headgear stretches (every 6 months), tubing degrades (every 3–6 months), and filters need regular replacement.
  • Travelling with PAP: Most modern machines are compact and TSA/airline-approved for carry-on. Carry a copy of your prescription. In India, a travel-sized inverter can ensure usage during train journeys.

What Compliance Data Tells Us

Global data shows that approximately 50–60% of patients are regularly using PAP therapy after one year. Indian studies suggest similar or slightly lower rates, with common barriers being cost, discomfort, lack of follow-up, and poor initial counselling. Patients who use PAP for at least 4 hours per night show significant reductions in blood pressure, daytime sleepiness, and cardiovascular event risk. The investment in perseverance during the first month pays dividends for years.
